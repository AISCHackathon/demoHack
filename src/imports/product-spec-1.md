Product: [Name TBD] — AI-Powered Fashion Production Assistant
The one-line pitch
Cursor for fashion production — AI that turns approved designs into manufacturer-ready documentation instantly.

The core problem
A designer finishes a garment concept. Now someone has to manually translate that creative vision into a tech pack — a structured, precise document a factory can actually use. That translation takes hours, requires technical knowledge most designers don't have, and is where the most costly errors happen. The F* Word and tools like it stop at "design approved." You start there.

What the product does
1. Image ingestion
User uploads a garment image — sketch, Procreate file, Midjourney render, photo of a sample, or export from The F* Word. The AI reads it using vision and immediately begins populating a structured tech pack. No form to fill out first, no category to select. It figures out what the garment is and starts working.
2. Auto-populated tech pack canvas
Split screen: image on the left, structured live document on the right. The AI fills in:

Garment category and silhouette classification
Front and back construction details (seams, closures, pockets, collar, cuffs, hem)
Fabric and material suggestions with weight and composition estimates
Trim details (zippers, buttons, labels, threads)
Colorways
Measurement table with estimated size ranges by garment type
Grading notes
Special instructions and construction callouts

Every field is editable inline. The AI pre-fills, the designer corrects. That's the Cursor moment — it does the hard work first, you just verify and refine.
3. Contextual AI suggestions
As you edit, the AI assists actively. You type "french seam at side" and it flags that seam allowance hasn't been specified. You change the fabric to a woven and it suggests updating construction methods accordingly. You add a zipper and it prompts for pull type, length, and color. It's reading everything you do and filling gaps in real time — not a chatbot, not a form wizard, but a collaborator that understands garment construction.
4. Annotation layer
User can click directly on the garment image to place callout markers that link to specific fields in the tech pack. So the collar annotation on the image connects to the collar spec row in the document. This is what makes it feel like a real production tool rather than a generator — the image and the spec are live-linked.
5. Brand memory
The platform remembers your brand's preferences over time. Your standard seam allowances, your go-to materials, your label placement rules, your size grade increments. Every new tech pack starts pre-loaded with your brand defaults, so the AI isn't starting from scratch each time. This is the compounding moat — the longer you use it, the more accurate and personalized the output.
6. Manufacturer-ready export
One-click PDF export that looks like a professional tech pack — not a generated document, but something a factory in Vietnam or Portugal would recognize and trust. Includes garment flat, all spec tables, annotation callouts, colorway swatches, and BOM draft. Also exports to Excel for factories that prefer it.

What it explicitly doesn't do (yet)

3D visualization (that's The F* Word's lane)
AI photoshoots or marketing assets
Supplier discovery or factory matching
Order management or PLM
Pattern making


Who it's for
Primary user is the emerging brand founder or freelance designer who is creative-first but technically under-resourced. They can't afford a full-time tech designer. They're currently either doing tech packs badly themselves, hiring expensive freelancers, or skipping them and eating factory errors.
Secondary user is the tech designer at a small studio who currently spends 80% of their time on repetitive data entry and wants to focus on the 20% that actually requires expertise.

The hackathon version
Three screens, one flow:
Upload image → AI populates tech pack canvas → export PDF
The annotation layer and brand memory are demo-able as UI even if partially faked. The AI population is real. The PDF export is real. That's enough to win.

The startup version
Same core, plus brand memory that compounds over time, annotation linking, and a BOM costing layer that estimates material costs based on specs. That's a 6-month roadmap after the hackathon validates the concept.
